From Dark Souls to Sekiro

30 May 2020

Following the success of the Dark Souls serie, From Software released Sekiro in 2019. It was not a surprise that it closely followed the the now beloved formula of its predecessor. The similitudes are many: cranked up difficulty compared to mainstream releases, impressive bosses, etc.

But there's also a world of difference between the two games, and I believe that it is there that Sekiro truly shine.

The Player Character

From the very beginning, the difference is obvious: the player is no longer able to create its own character but is instead incarnating the eponymous Sekiro. Beyond the cosmetic aspect

Old Stuff

Sekiro: Shadows Die Twice closely follows the footsteps of the Dark Souls serie, but adds its own twist to it. One of the biggest change to the formula is the way the player's character can be developped.

In Dark Souls, the progression of the player's character came mainly from two factors: equipment (mostly found by exploring the world) and stats (raised by spending souls, mostly found on dead enemies). The various equipment pieces could also be reinforced by spending souls and crafting materials.

While the overall system was easy to understand, the fact that every stats behaved differently and that every piece of equipment had different flaws and benefits and could in turn be reinforced in different way to provide additional effects made it difficult for the player to fully understand what would be the effect of each decision on the game.

The sandbox aspect of Dark Souls character progression was very interesting, but From Software decided to go for a different, tighter system for Sekiro. While the decision to have the character progression be more restrained in Sekiro makes sense from the point of view of the story coherence (since you are no longer playing as an anonymous undead but rather as a character with a pre-established story), I believe it is also interesting to consider what could have triggered those changes from the point of view of the game design.

Like Dark Souls, Sekiro features several layer of character progression, but those layers are very differents from each other. There are three main layers: stats increase, skill trees, and the prosthetic tool upgrade tree. Let's see how each of those layer fit into the overall design of the game.

Stats increase

Sekiro has an extremely simple stat system. Your character only has two stats: Attack Power (how much damage your attacks do) and Physical Attributes (how much health and posture you have). Attack Power is raised by one each time you kill a boss, Physical Attributes is raised by one each time you gather four Prayer Beads.

Obviously the complexity has been reduced from the eight different stats in Dark Souls. But it should also be noted that this system has other consequences:

  • The player can no longer choose which stat to raise.
  • The player can no longer farm to raise stats.

Given the lack of variability of this system, it is easy for the developpers to roughly predict what will be the stats of the player at any given point in the game. This is interesting because it allows the developpers to tweak some values (such as boss health and posture) to be more precisely balanced to reflect the capabilities of the player.

There is still a small progress margin available to the player. For example, if a player is stuck on a boss, there is always the option to hunt for more Prayer Beads in order to increase their health and posture.

Skill trees

Over the course of the game, the player will unlock several skill trees. Each skill can be unlocked for a given amount of experience, obtained by killing enemies. Unlike the stats system, it is harder to predict what skills the player will have at any point in the game: different players might unlock different skills, some players might decide to farm experience, etc.

A skill tree in Sekiro

However, it is worth noting that some of the most important skills (such as the Mikiri Counter) are located at the beginning of the first skill trees the player will unlock. Therefore, it is reasonable to assume that a great majority of players will have access to the same core set of fundamental skills at some point early enough in the game.

Moreover, the game makes it very obvious that some skill are almost mandatory and should be unlocked as soon as possible. For example, toward the beginning of the game, the player has to scale the massive Ashina Castle. As the player ascends the structure using the grapling hook, enemies will use projectile against him. As the player will be in mid-air most of the time, those projectiles cannot be blocked unless the "Mid-air Deflection" skill is acquired. This skill will be extremely handy in the immediately following boss fight.

Prosthetic tool upgrade

The third and last mean for the player to upgrade their character is the prosthetic tool upgrade tree. Prosthetic tools are useful and can make some parts of the game easier, but they are also dispensable. As such, the game gives more freedom and less guidance regarding which upgrade to pick.

I like to think of prosthetic tools as a way for the player to experiment with the game in a way that is reminiscent of Dark Souls. Some tools might have unexpected use (such as the Loaded Spear allowing you to perform a special attack on the Headless Ape boss) or might be especially effective against specific enemies (such as the Sabimaru being powerful against Okami warriors).

It is also worth mentioning that the rarest crafting materials are strictly limited in one playthrough. Therefore, different players will end up with different incomplete upgrade trees depending on their favorite tools.

Conclusion

As you can see, each of the path used to develop the player character has a different goal. In contrast to Dark Souls, it seems like From Software tried to have more control on how the player would progress through the game.

The stats system is a basic but effective way to soft-lock the player into following the "right" path, and to ensure that re-exploring early-game areas is easier than the first time around. Given how the combat mechanics work, it still allows skilled player to go off the beaten path.

The skill system allows the player to be more engaged with the game, as you are constantly acquiring experience points, you know you will have a new skill as reward sooner or later.

Finally, prosthetic tools are a nice extra for players that want to experiment more, or that found a specific challenge too difficult and want to make it easier.

What could be improved?

The skill system is the weakest one in my opinion, because once you acquired the main passive skills, the active skills don't feel really useful (especially since you can only equip one at a time). It especially shows if you are going into NG+ and try to complete the skill tree. I believe it could be fixed simply by replacing some active skills by passive skills, which provide a bigger incentive for the player. Some interesting additions would have been:

  • The Terror status is extremely dangerous (compared to the Burn and Poison status) and the enemies able to inflict it are annoying to fight. A skill increasing your resistance to Terror available in the late game would be nice.
  • The Unseen Aid mechanic is very punishing and rarely feel fair. A skill allowing you to raise the Unseen Aid triggering chance would be a way to mitigate that.

The active skills could be instead found by killing mini-bosses or achieving other in-game challenges.
